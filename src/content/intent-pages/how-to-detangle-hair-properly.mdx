## Understanding How To Detangle Hair Properly

Mechanical trauma—the physical act of roughly pulling, ripping, or poorly brushing tangled hair—is responsible for a staggering amount of the macroscopic breakage, split ends, and "thinning" clients complain about in the salon.

A tangle, or a knot, occurs when the lifted, jagged cuticles of two individual hair strands act like velcro, latching onto one another. If you take a standard hairbrush and violently yank it down from your root through the tangle, you are not untangling the hair; you are physically snapping the strands in half to force the brush through. 

Detangling must be viewed as a delicate, strategic untying of microscopic knots, rather than a forceful plowing operation.

## Our Professional Advice

Whether your hair is perfectly straight, heavily bleached, or tightly coiled into 4C spirals, there is a universal, non-negotiable methodology to detangling that prevents massive breakage and thinning.

### 1. The Right State: Wet vs Dry
The golden rule of detangling depends entirely on your specific hair texture:
- **If you have Straight or Wavy hair:** Only detangle when the hair is *completely dry* or 90% dry using a paddle brush. Straight hair is at its absolute weakest when soaking wet; the hydrogen bonds break down, making the hair hyper-elastic and incredibly prone to snapping.
- **If you have Curly or Coily hair:** *Never* brush your hair dry. You will snap the tight curl structures and create an explosive cloud of frizz. You must only detangle when the hair is soaking wet, drenched in the shower, and heavily coated in a slippery, thick deep conditioner.

### 2. The Tool Selection
Throw away the cheap, hard plastic bristle brushes.
- Invest in a flexible "wet detangler" brush (which features incredibly soft, bending bristles that yield to tension rather than ripping through it) or a high-quality wide-tooth seamless comb. 

### 3. The Bottom-Up Technique
This is the most critical professional secret. The vast majority of people place their brush at the root (the scalp) and yank downwards. This catches a small knot near the root and shoves it all the way down the hair shaft, combining it with every other knot until you have an un-brushable, massive dreadlock at the bottom of your hair.
- **The Correct Way:** Grab your hair like a ponytail in your fist to absorb tension. Start brushing only the bottom two inches (the tips) of your hair. Once the tips are clear, move the brush two inches higher, brushing down through the now-clear tips. Continue working systematically upwards to the root.

If your hair is severely, chronically matting and tangling despite proper care, it means your cuticles are heavily damaged.
